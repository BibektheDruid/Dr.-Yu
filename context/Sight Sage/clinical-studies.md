## Clinical Studies & Scientific Evidence

This document captures every clinical study, trial, statistic, and scientific citation that SightSage (sightsage.com/en-ca) references across its product pages, its "Clinical Studies" page, blog, and ingredient/science pages — together with a verification note on whether a real, traceable citation is actually provided.

### Summary of findings

- SightSage has **one genuine proprietary clinical trial of its own product**: a company-sponsored, completed, randomized double-blind trial of its blueberry gummy for dry eye, registered on ClinicalTrials.gov as **NCT05027087 ("PLUM")**. This is the only study on the site that actually tests a SightSage product.
- The bulk of the site's "science" is **ingredient-level citations** (blueberry/bilberry/anthocyanin, goji/lutein/zeaxanthin) drawn from third-party literature, concentrated on the `/pages/blueberries` science page and the goji blog post. Several of these point to real, peer-reviewed PubMed/PMC papers, especially in the goji blog.
- **Most product pages (SightC, Hair Pro, Adaptogen-X, the gummy product page itself) make health claims with NO citations** — only ingredient descriptions, founder authority, and customer reviews.
- The dedicated **`/pages/our-clinical-study` page is JavaScript-rendered**; its visible body could not be fully extracted by automated fetch. What is detectable in its markup is a Typeform survey embed and a link to the ClinicalTrials.gov record NCT05027087. No journal citations, author lists, or numeric results were retrievable from that page's static HTML, so the rich evidence actually lives on the blueberries science page and the goji blog, not on the page literally named "Clinical Studies."

### 1. SightSage's own proprietary clinical trial (the only study testing a SightSage product)

**Trial: "The Effect of a Novel Blueberry Supplement on Dry Eye Disease (PLUM)" — NCT05027087**
- **What is claimed / what it is:** A trial of a "novel blueberry supplement" (blueberry-powder gummy bears vs. placebo) for dry eye disease. Supports the **Blueberry Gummies** (and the brand's overall "backed by clinical research" positioning).
- **Cited source:** ClinicalTrials.gov record NCT05027087, linked from `/pages/our-clinical-study`.
- **Details (verified on ClinicalTrials.gov):** Sponsor = **SightSage Foods and Nutrition Inc.** (industry). Interventional, randomized, **double-blind**, parallel-group, listed as Phase 3. **Enrollment: 68 adults (18+)** with documented dry eye. Primary outcomes: change in OSDI score and tear break-up time over 4 weeks. **Status: Completed (Jan 2023).**
- **Verification note:** REAL and traceable — this is a genuine registered trial sponsored by SightSage itself. Caveat: as of retrieval, **no results were posted** on the registry, and I found **no peer-reviewed publication** of the PLUM results. So the underlying data is not yet independently verifiable; the brand is relying on a registered-but-unpublished company trial.

### 2. Goji / SightC eye-health evidence (blog: "Goji Berries and Dry Eyes")

The goji blog post (`/blogs/eye-health/goji-berries-and-dry-eyes...`) is the most heavily cited page on the site and supports the **SightC** dry-eye / goji product. Each claim below links to a real NCBI/PubMed identifier.

| Claim | Cited source | Verification note |
|---|---|---|
| Goji berry extract significantly reduced dry eye symptoms in rats (doses 250/350/500 mg/kg; Schirmer, TBUT, fluorescein staining) | "2017 animal study," PMC5780158 | Real PMC link provided. Animal (rat) study, not human. |
| Milk-based goji formulation raised plasma zeaxanthin **+26%** and antioxidant capacity **+57%** in elderly after 90 days | PubMed 21169874 | Real PubMed link. (This is the older Bucheli/Ningxia goji study.) |
| **28 g goji berries 5×/week for 90 days significantly increased macular pigment density** in adults 45–65 | PMC8708314 | REAL, verified: *Li X et al., "Goji Berry Intake Increases Macular Pigment Optical Density in Healthy Adults: A Randomized Pilot Trial," Nutrients 2021.* 27 participants; significant MPOD increase (p=0.029 and p=0.044). UC Davis; registered NCT03983525. Strongest goji citation on the site. |
| Goji polysaccharide eye drops improved tear production/corneal integrity in mice (0.625/2.5/12.5 mg/ml, 3×/day, 10 days) | PubMed 35360713 | Real PubMed link. Animal (mouse) study. |
| Meta-analysis: goji+Rehmannia pills + eye drops beat eye drops alone for dry eye — **1,222 patients, 15 RCTs** | PubMed 32664082 | Real PubMed link. Note: tests a TCM pill combination, not SightC specifically. |
| 15 g goji provides the daily recommended 3 mg/day zeaxanthin | PubMed 15705234 | Real PubMed link (nutritional composition reference). |

**Verification note (goji overall):** This is the site's best-sourced area — multiple real, clickable PubMed/PMC citations. Important caveats: several are **animal studies** (rats, mice), some test **TCM formulas rather than SightC itself**, and the strongest human study (MPOD pilot) measured macular pigment, not dry-eye relief. The product pages for SightC and the gummies themselves carry **no citations** — the evidence lives only in the blog.

### 3. Blueberry / anthocyanin eye-health evidence (science page: `/pages/blueberries`)

Supports the **Blueberry Gummies**. This page lists claims attributed to named journals but **provides no article titles, authors, years, sample sizes, percentages, or hyperlinks** — only the journal name.

| Claim | Cited "source" (journal name only) | Verification note |
|---|---|---|
| Blueberries booster collagen structure in the retina / antioxidant vision protection | *Current Research in Nutrition and Food Science* | Journal named; no specific paper, no link. Not independently verifiable as stated. |
| Higher dietary flavonoid intake may lower risk of cataracts | *The American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* | Journal named only; no citation/link. |
| Cohort of women 45+ associated blueberry intake with reduced AMD risk; anthocyanidins inhibit growth of extra blood vessels in advancing AMD | *Advances in Nutrition* | Journal named only; no citation/link. |
| Blueberries improve retinal abnormalities, increase capillary resistance, slow lens opacity and myopia progression | *Journal of Food Science* | Journal named only; no citation/link. |
| Blueberries protect against photooxidation of A2E (autofluorescence pigment) | *Acta Ophthalmologica* | Journal named only; no citation/link. |
| Blueberry anthocyanins promote renewal of cultured corneal limbal epithelial cells | *Oxidative Medicine and Cellular Longevity* | Journal named only; no citation/link (in-vitro cell study). |
| 50 mg/day black-currant anthocyanin for 6 months improved ocular blood flow and visual field in glaucoma patients | Hirosaki University | Institution named (this matches a real Ohguro et al. black-currant glaucoma study); no link provided on page. |

**Verification note (blueberry page):** These point to **real journals** and several correspond to genuine published work, but as presented they are **partial citations** (journal/institution name with no title, authors, year, or URL). A reader cannot click through or confirm them from the site. Quality of sourcing here is markedly weaker than the goji blog.

**Related external literature SightSage's positioning leans on (not necessarily cited on-site):** *Yu WY, Chan LYL, Chung A, Lee PH, Woo GC, "Bilberry-containing supplements on severe dry eye disease in young and middle-aged adults," Frontiers in Nutrition 2023* (PMC9892183) — a real examiner-masked pilot (24 subjects; 600 mg bilberry / 150 mg anthocyanin + DHA; OSDI improved 11.21 vs 0.95 points). Note the lead author surname **"Yu"** overlaps with the SightSage founders, but the paper does not name SightSage/Fovera. Verification note: real peer-reviewed study, but not cited as a SightSage product trial.

### 6. Brand-level / founder authority claims (not studies)

- Homepage: products "Backed by 40+ years of clinical research" (elsewhere "20+ years" / "30+ years" — the figure is inconsistent across pages).
- Founder page: Dr. Weidong Yu (TCM) — "20+ years of research and clinical practice," treated "over 1,000 patients with Retinitis Pigmentosa," author of "30+ publications"; Kathy Yu/Zhang, Registered Dietitian / 30+ years TCM.
- **Verification note:** These are **authority/experience assertions, not clinical evidence.** No patient outcome data, no published trial, and no link to the "30+ publications" is provided. The year counts conflict between pages.

### Overall verification verdict

- **Genuinely substantiated by a real SightSage trial:** Blueberry Gummies / dry eye — via registered trial NCT05027087 (PLUM), though results are unpublished/unposted.
- **Supported by real third-party citations (ingredient-level):** Goji/SightSage eye claims (good PubMed/PMC links, but several animal/TCM-formula studies) and, to a weaker degree, blueberry claims (journals named without retrievable citations).
- **No scientific evidence cited at all:** Hair Pro, Adaptogen-X, weight-loss products, and all main product pages (SightC and the gummy page carry claims with zero on-page citations).
- **Caveats for any downstream use:** (1) the page literally titled "Clinical Studies" is JS-rendered and surfaces mainly a survey + the trial registry link, not detailed citations; (2) the brand's "40+ years of clinical research" tagline is inconsistent across pages and is not a study; (3) much eye-health evidence is ingredient-extrapolated (and sometimes from animals or different formulations) rather than tests of the finished SightSage product.
